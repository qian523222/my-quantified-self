My 30 Days: How Do My Emotions Shift When Screens Take Over My Time?

A Quantified Self Experiment on the Relationship Between Step Count, Screen Time, and Emotions

Data Collection Period: March 1–30, 2026

Introduction — Why Am I Tracking My Data?

In the digital age, it seems that all our behaviors, hobbies, and consumption patterns are being recorded and analyzed. For example, mobile applications track our preferences based on our usage habits and continuously recommend content that appears to be most suitable for us. This inevitably makes me wonder: what kind of person am I behind this data? Can analyzing such data help me understand myself more deeply? I also wanted to know how my time is actually used.

I began to notice that some days I felt unexpectedly low, even when I had more free time. At present, my course schedule is relatively concentrated, and most of my time is self-managed, including completing coursework, reading materials, and meeting deadlines. In theory, this learning model gives me a higher degree of control over my time. However, how is my “free time” actually used? Am I getting enough physical activity? Is my screen use gradually becoming excessive? More importantly, how do these daily behaviors affect my mood score?

To answer these questions, I conducted a 30-day quantitative self-experiment. From March 1 to March 30, 2026, I recorded three core variables:

  • Step count: representing the level of physical activity
  • Screen time: representing the intensity of digital life (hours per day)
  • Mood score: rated on a scale from 1 to 7 (1 = very low, 7 = very happy)

This study aims to explore the relationships among these variables and address a central question: how do my daily choices shape my mood in a relatively independent graduate learning environment?

According to the data, over these 30 days, my average daily step count was approximately 7,800 steps, my average screen time was over 10 hours per day, and my average mood score was 5.4 out of 7. However, these averages conceal significant fluctuations: step counts ranged from 362 to 23,305, and mood score varied between 3 and 7. These fluctuations suggest underlying patterns that are worthy of further analysis.

Methodology — How Were the Data Collected and Analyzed?

All the data used in this study were derived from daily self-reports over a 30-day period. Step counts were automatically recorded by the built-in health tracking application of the iPhone and the screen time was collected through the monitoring function. Mood scores were recorded through subjective self-assessment on a scale of 1 to 7 each night before bed (of which 1 means that the mood is very bad and 7 means that the mood is very happy). All data were organized in Microsoft Excel, and data visualizations were produced using Flourish, including line charts, bar charts and scatter charts to show the trends and relationships between variables.

Overall Trends — My March Activity Trajectory

As shown in Figure 1, my physical activity levels are highly inconsistent. On March 16, I reached 23,305 steps - on that day, I went to Dublin to submit Danish visa information and spent most of the day walking around the city. On March 18, the step count plummeted to 362 steps, and I stayed in my dormitory with almost no movement. After carefully reviewing last month's itinerary, I found that my peak steps often occur on two days: one is the day when I have classes and go to the gym; typically before or after class, as I often incorporate gym visits into my routine since the gym was conveniently on my way; the other is the days when I work part-time and exercise on weekends, and the steps are usually more than 10,000 steps. On the contrary, the low step count often occurs in "unscheduled days" with no curriculum and no exercise plan. This figure reveals that for me, step counts were not passively accumulated through commuting, but rather the result of active choices. When I didn't plan exercise or outdoor activities, my physical activity decreased sharply.

The Impact of Time Structure: An Analysis of Differences Between Weekdays and Weekends

After noticing these obvious fluctuations, I began to think about whether these changes occurred randomly or were affected by my daily schedule. Just observing the change trends over time was not sufficient to explain these patterns. Therefore, I adopted a categorical approach to compare and analyze different types of days

In this data set, dates are categorized as “weekdays” and “weekends.” Weekdays include Monday through Friday, while weekends only include Saturdays and Sundays. This classification is based on the standard calendar structure and is used to examine how time structure influences patterns in the data.
