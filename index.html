My 30 Days: How Do My Emotions Shift When Screens Take Over My Time?

A Quantified Self Experiment on the Relationship Between Step Count, Screen Time, and Emotions

Data Collection Period: March 1–30, 2026

Name: Yang Qian | Course: Humanities and New Technologies: Tools and Methodologies

Introduction — Why Am I Tracking My Data?

In the digital age, it seems that all our behaviors, hobbies, and consumption patterns are being recorded and analyzed. For example, mobile applications track our preferences based on our usage habits and continuously recommend content that appears to be most suitable for us. This inevitably makes me wonder: what kind of person am I behind this data? Can analyzing such data help me understand myself more deeply? I also wanted to know how my time is actually used.

At present, my course schedule is relatively concentrated, and most of my time is self-managed, including completing coursework, reading materials, and meeting deadlines. In theory, this learning model gives me a higher degree of control over my time. However, how is my “free time” actually used? Am I getting enough physical activity? Is my screen use gradually becoming excessive? More importantly, how do these daily behaviors affect my mood score?

To answer these questions, I conducted a 30-day quantitative self-experiment. From March 1 to March 30, 2026, I recorded three core variables:

  • Step count: representing the level of physical activity
  • Screen time: representing the intensity of digital life (hours per day)
  • Mood score: rated on a scale from 1 to 7 (1 = very low, 7 = very happy)

This study aims to explore the relationships among these variables and address a central question: how do my daily choices shape my mood in a relatively independent graduate learning environment?

According to the data, over these 30 days, my average daily step count was approximately 7,800 steps, my average screen time was over 10 hours per day, and my average mood score was 5.4 out of 7. However, these averages conceal significant fluctuations: step counts ranged from 362 to 23,305, and mood score varied between 3 and 7. These fluctuations suggest underlying patterns that are worthy of further analysis.

Methodology — How Were the Data Collected and Analyzed?

All the data used in this study were derived from daily self-reports over a 30-day period. Step counts were automatically recorded by the built-in health tracking application of the iPhone and the screen time was collected through the monitoring function. Mood scores were recorded through subjective self-assessment on a scale of 1 to 7 each night before bed (of which 1 means that the mood is very bad and 7 means that the mood is very happy). All data were organized in Microsoft Excel, and data visualization is produced using Flourish, including line charts, bar charts and scatter charts to show the trends and relationships between variables.

Overall Trends — My March Activity Trajectory

As shown in Figure 1, my physical activity levels are highly inconsistent. On March 16, I reached 23,305 steps - on that day, I went to Dublin to submit Danish visa information and spent most of the day walking around the city. On March 18, the step count plummeted to 362 steps, and I stayed in my dormitory with almost no movement. March 5 (12,500 steps), March 8 (13,000 steps) and March 30 (15,500 steps) also showed peak values. After carefully reviewing last month's itinerary, I found that my peak steps often occur on two days: one is the day when I have classes and go to the gym; typically before or after class, as I often incorporate gym visits into my routine since the gym was conveniently on my way; the other is the days when I work part-time and exercise on weekends, and the steps are usually more than 10,000 steps. On the contrary, the low step count (2,500 steps on March 4, 3,000 steps on March 10, and 3,000 steps on March 24) often occurs in "unscheduled days" with no curriculum and no exercise plan. This figure reveals that for me, step counts were not passively accumulated through commuting, but rather the result of active choices. When I didn't plan exercise or outdoor activities, my physical activity will decrease sharply.

The Impact of Time Structure: An Analysis of Differences Between Weekdays and Weekends

After noticing these obvious fluctuations, I began to think about whether these changes occurred randomly or were affected by my daily schedule. Just observing the change trends over time was not sufficient to explain these patterns. Therefore, I adopted a categorical approach to compare and analyze different types of days.

In this data set, dates are categorized as “weekdays” and “weekends.” Weekdays include Monday through Friday, while weekends only include Saturdays and Sundays. This classification is based on the standard calendar structure and is used to examine how time structure influences patterns in the data.

In terms of step count, the average step count on weekdays is about 8,012, while on weekends it is about 6,414 - which means that my overall activity level on weekdays was higher than that on weekends.

This difference is worth further exploration. The high step count does not result from commuting to classes (because the teaching building is close to my dormitory), but is more likely to be related to the structure of my daily routine. When I need to go out for classes on weekdays, I often include fitness and other activities in the daily schedule.

In other words, classes function as an "anchor" for behavior to some extent, allowing me to organize other activities around it more naturally. For graduate students, this "anchor effect" was especially important - when the external structure is relatively scarce, individuals need to actively create daily structure to maintain their own activity level.

In contrast, although there is more free time on weekends, it is also more likely to lack structure. If I don't arrange exercise or go out in advance, I tend to reduce the amount of activity, which leads to a lower step count.

In terms of screen time, the average duration is about 11 hours on weekdays and about 9 hours on weekends.

This result shows that my screen time is largely driven by academic tasks. On weekdays, I need to complete course-related readings and assignments —tasks that are almost entirely dependent on electronic devices. Therefore, although I still maintain a certain amount of physical activity during these days, the screen time remained high.

In contrast, the screen time on weekends has decreased significantly. This does not mean that I have consciously reduced the use of electronic devices, but rather that the way I allocate my time changes. On weekends, I prefer to engage in part-time work, sports or outdoor activities; these physical activities substitute for screen time to a certain extent, thus naturally reducing the overall duration.

This pattern further suggests that my screen time does not depend entirely on "self-control", but is largely influenced by the structure of daily tasks.

In terms of mood fluctuations, there is also a clear difference between weekdays and weekends. On weekdays, my average mood score is about 5; and on weekends, this value rises to about 6. That is to say, when the academic pressure is reduced and the free time increases, I can arrange my study and daily life more independently, thereby improving my mood score to a certain extent.

However, this increase did not reach the level of "very happy" (7 points). This shows that just having more free time does not automatically bring higher emotional well-being. On the contrary, freedom is a double-edged sword. When I have complete control over time, if I lack active planning, I am more likely to fall into a state of inactivity, excessive electronic screen use and insufficient physical activity.

Although complete freedom reduces pressure, it also places greater demands on self-management. What I need is not "more freedom", but "building my own structure" in freedom. This is likely to be one of the key factors affecting my daily mood score.

Key Relationships — Exercise and Mood, Screen Time and Mood

This is the core of this analysis. I use scatter plots to explore two key questions: Does more exercise lead to better mood? Does more screen time lead to worse mood?

Finding 1: There is a positive correlation between step count and mood

It can be seen from the scatter plot that there appears to be a positive correlation between my steps and my mood. Although there are some fluctuations in the data points, the overall trend suggests: "The higher my steps, the better my mood".

Specifically, when step count is less than 5,000, my mood score on that day rarely exceeds 5; in contrast, when the step count exceeds 10,000, my mood was generally more stable, typically at 6 or above. In addition, the sharp contrast between March 18 (362 steps, corresponding to the mood score of 3) and March 16 (23,305 steps, corresponding to the mood score of 7) also further strengthens this trend.

For me, this discovery is particularly meaningful. Since my steps mainly come from active exercise (such as fitness or part-time activities) rather than passive commuting behavior, it shows that step count reflects my behavioral choices to a certain extent. When I feel low or encounter setbacks in my study, active exercise may be an effective way to regulate my mood. Exercise not only helps to improve my physical condition, but also provides me with a "psychological buffer" to distance myself from academic pressure.

Finding 2: Screen time is negatively correlated with emotional well-being

As shown in the scatter plot, it can be observed that when my screen time increases, my mood tended to decline. Although there are some fluctuations in the data, the overall trend remains clear. When the screen time is less than 8 hours, my mood is mostly maintained above 6 points; when the screen time is between 10 and 12 hours, my mood score is mainly concentrated around 6; and when the screen time is more than 12 hours, my mood is rarely maintained at a high level.

Looking back on these days with high screen time, it usually means rushing papers, dealing with deadlines, or reading academic literature for a long time. In these cases, not only does my screen time increase, but my stress, fatigue and cognitive load will also increase. Therefore, screen time itself may not be the only reason for my declining mood, but it often corresponds to a state of "high intensity".

When I combined this result with the step data and analyzed it, I found a particularly interesting contrast. For example, the screen time on March 16 and March 18 is almost exactly the same (about 11.2 hours), but the corresponding mood score is 7 and 3 respectively. The key difference between the two is the step count: the former is 23,305 steps, while the latter is only 362 steps.

This made me realize that exercise may be a key factor in determining the mood score when the screen time is similar. In other words, even if you can't reduce screen time, as long as you can maintain a certain level of physical activity, my mood may still be restored to a more positive state.

According to the data, the average mood score on Wednesday was 4.2, which is significantly lower than other days. Looking back at my schedule， Wednesdays typically had neither classes nor a fixed exercise plan. Although the day seems to have the most "free time", it is precisely because of the lack of structure that I am more likely to fall into a cycle of inactivity, excessive screen use and declining mood. In contrast, Friday's mood score was the highest (about 6.4). Although there was no class schedule on Friday, I usually arrange part-time work or exercise. These activities themselves can improve mood.

This discovery made me realize that the key to the problem is not whether I have time or not, but whether that time is structured. If I can take the initiative to arrange some activities on Wednesday - even if it's just going to the gym for an hour or taking a walk with friends - I may be able to break this cycle of "midweek lows" and maintain a more stable mood score during the week.

Conclusions and Action Steps — What Did I Learn?

Through this 30-day quantified self-experiment, I gradually realized that my mood score is not determined by a single factor, but shaped by a combination of behavioral factors.

First of all, I did not have full control over screen time, which is largely affected by academic tasks and work. As a graduate student, prolonged use of electronic devices is often unavoidable. Therefore, rather than simply aiming to reduce screen time, it may be more effective to focus on how to maintain physical and mental balance in a high-screen environment.

Secondly, physical activity plays an important stabilizing role in regulating mood. Whether it is daily exercise or going for a walk, physical engagement can improve the mood score to a certain extent and offset the negative impact of sedentary behavior.

In addition, this experiment also made me realize that in the absence of a clear plan, free time does not necessarily bring a better experience; on the contrary, it is more likely to lead to a decline in activity levels and emotional fluctuations. This shows that it is especially important to take the initiative to establish a daily structure in a highly autonomous learning environment.

Based on the above findings, I plan to make the following adjustments in my daily life:

  • Proactively schedule outdoor or physical activity in the middle of the week (for example, Wednesday) to avoid emotional fluctuations caused by the lack of structure.
  • Schedule mandatory breaks after prolonged screen use, such as taking a short walk or doing simple exercises.
  • Set minimum activity standards (such as daily step targets) to maintain the basic level of physical activity.
  • On days when there is no course schedule, plan at least one offline activity in advance to establish a behavioral anchor.

The purpose of these adjustments is not to completely change my current lifestyle, but to establish a more stable behavioral rhythm in the existing learning structure.

Limitations and Reflections

This study has several limitations:

First of all, the sample size was not large, and the data were collected over only 30 consecutive days. It is difficult to fully represent long-term behavioral habits, and increases the likelihood of being influenced by short-term special events.

Secondly, the range of variables examined was relatively limited. Mood scores are actually affected by many factors, such as sleep quality, social interactions, etc., but this study only focuses on the step count and screen time, which resulted in limited explanatory power.

In addition, the mood score is obtained by subjective self-assessment, which may be affected by memory bias or daily mood fluctuations.

Finally, this study is highly individualized, and the conclusions drawn only apply to me and may not be generalizable to other populations.

Despite these shortcomings, this study gave me a valuable understanding of my behavior patterns, and also provided a reference for adjustments to my lifestyle in the future.
